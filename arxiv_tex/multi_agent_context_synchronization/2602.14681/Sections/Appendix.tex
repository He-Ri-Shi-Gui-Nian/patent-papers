\section{Details of Methodology}
We introduce some additional technical details in this section, related to the specific implementations.
\subsection{Details of Optimization Objectives}
\label{app: loss}

Equation~(\ref{eq: 18}), (\ref{eq: 20}), and (\ref{eq: 14}) introduce three optimization objectives of \ourmethod. Among them, Equation~(\ref{eq: 18}), (\ref{eq: 20}) are optimized as reward models. Inituitively, we merge them as $\mathcal{L}_{\text{utility}}$:
\begin{gather}
    -\frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M e^{\mathcal{R}_{\text{sta}}}\cdot u(\mathcal{G}^m(\mathcal{Q}))\cdot \nabla_\Theta({P(\mathcal{G}^m)}),
\end{gather}
where $\mathcal{R}_{\text{sta}} \in \mathbb{R}^-$, so that $e^{\mathcal{R}_{\text{sta}}} \in [0,1]$ can be treated as a confidence level, which reflects the uncertainty of the scheduling trajectory. And the total loss function $\mathcal{L}$ is:
\begin{gather}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{utility}} + \gamma \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{reg}}
\end{gather}


\subsection{Details of Retrieve-Augment}
\label{app: rag}

To efficiently manage temporal node states under memory constraints, we introduce our retrieval mechanism. Unlike traditional graph stores, our memory $\mathcal{M}$ with capacity $L$ stores latent tensor representations. Each item $m_i \in \mathcal{M}$ is a tuple $(\mathbf{k}_i, \mathcal{H}_i, c_i, u_i, \alpha_i)$, where $\mathbf{k}_i$ is the query embedding key, $\mathcal{H}_i$ is the sequence of node feature tensors (values), $c_i$ is the computational cost, $u_i$ is the uncertainty metric (i.e., $\mathcal{R}_{\text{sta}}$), and $\alpha_i$ tracks access frequency.

\textbf{Retrieval and Update.} 
Given a query $\mathbf{q}$, we retrieve the top-$k$ items maximizing the dot-product similarity $\mathbf{k}_i^\top \mathbf{q}$. To maintain the most valuable information within the fixed capacity $L$, we employ a value-based eviction policy. When the buffer is full, a new candidate $m_{new}$ replaces the item with the minimum retention score $S(m)$ if $S(m_{new}) > \min_{j} S(m_j)$. The score $S(m)$ balances reliability, efficiency, and popularity:

\begin{equation}
    S(m_i) = \frac{1 + \log(\alpha_i + 1)}{c_i \cdot |u_i| + \epsilon}
\end{equation}

where $\epsilon$ is a smoothing term. This policy prioritizes items with low uncertainty ($|u_i| \to 0$) and low computational cost ($c_i$), while logarithmically scaling with access frequency to preserve useful knowledge without saturation.

\section{More Related Works}

\subsection{Time-Series and Spatio-Temporal Data Analysis}
The analysis of time-series data is a fundamental challenge in numerous fields, with recent research increasingly shifting from classical statistical models to deep learning-based solutions. A comprehensive survey of these methods from a channel strategy perspective highlights the diversity of approaches for multivariate forecasting \citep{qiu2025comprehensive}. A primary focus in this area is the development of robust models for long-sequence time-series forecasting. Innovations in training paradigms and model architecture, such as the use of a quadratic direct forecast approach \citep{wang2026iclrqdf} and the exploration of selective representation spaces from a patch perspective \citep{wu2025srsnet}, have been proposed to enhance prediction accuracy. The move towards generative approaches is also notable, with models aiming for universal multimodal forecasting capabilities \citep{wu2026aurora} or employing frameworks like Koopman-Kalman enhanced Variational AutoEncoders for probabilistic forecasting \citep{wu2025k2vae}.

Concurrently, significant attention has been given to the design of objective functions to better guide the learning process. Methodologies such as learning to forecast in the frequency domain \citep{wang2025fredf}, employing transformed label alignment \citep{wang2025timeo1}, and using decomposition-based loss functions \citep{qiu2025dbloss} have proven effective. Other work focuses on improving the alignment between the model's output and the ground truth, for instance, by minimizing the Wasserstein distance between their joint distributions \citep{wang2026iclrdistdf}.

The field also addresses specific complexities inherent in time-series data. For multivariate series, methods like dual clustering have been introduced to enhance forecasting \citep{qiu2025duet}, while other approaches leverage dual causal networks to effectively incorporate exogenous variables \citep{qiu2025dag}. For irregular time series, there has been a call to rethink complex models in favor of simple yet effective baselines \citep{liu2025rethinking}. Beyond forecasting, related tasks such as time-series imputation have also benefited from advanced techniques like optimal transport \citep{wang2025optimal}. The parallel task of anomaly detection is also evolving, with methods using channel-aware frequency patching \citep{wu2024catch}.

Given the rapid proliferation of new methods, the community has emphasized the need for automation, standardized benchmarking, and accessible tools. Efforts towards zero-shot joint neural architecture and hyperparameter search \citep{AutoCTS++} and fully automated forecasting systems \citep{wu2024fully} aim to streamline the modeling pipeline. To ensure rigorous evaluation, comprehensive and fair benchmarking frameworks have been developed for both forecasting \citep{qiu2024tfb} and anomaly detection \citep{qiu2025tab}. Furthermore, the development of accessible toolkits aims to make time series forecasting easy for a broader audience \citep{qiu2025easytime}. The practical impact of these sophisticated frameworks is demonstrated in their application to critical real-world problems, including the development of accurate and interpretable systems for ensuring industrial security \citep{wang2023accurate}.

\subsection{Controllable Spatio-Temporal Content Generation}
The principles of modeling sequential data are also central to the generation and manipulation of high-dimensional spatio-temporal content like video. As detailed in a recent survey on the topic \citep{ma2025controllable}, a major trend in this field is controllable video generation. Researchers have developed a variety of methods that allow for fine-grained control over the synthesized output using intuitive user inputs. For example, video generation can be guided by pose information, enabling animation from a sequence of poses even when models are trained on pose-free videos \citep{ma2024followpose}. Similarly, motion transfer can be achieved through efficient spatial-temporal decoupled finetuning \citep{ma2025followyourmotion}. The scope of control also extends to more direct interactions, such as animating a specific image region based on a simple click and motion prompt \citep{ma2025followyourclick} or creating expressive portrait animations guided by emojis \citep{ma2024followyouremoji}. Subsequent work has focused on making these animation processes more efficient \citep{ma2025followfaster}. This paradigm of control is also being applied to video editing and content creation, with methods that use control handle transformations for precise edits \citep{ma2023magicstick} and leverage video inpainting to empower 4D creation \citep{ma2025followcreation}.
