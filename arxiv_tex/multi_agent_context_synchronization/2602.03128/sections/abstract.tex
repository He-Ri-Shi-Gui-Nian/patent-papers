\begin{abstract}
Multi-agent LLM frameworks are widely used to accelerate the development of agent systems powered by large language models (LLMs). These frameworks impose distinct architectural structures that govern how agents interact, store information, and coordinate tasks. However, their impact on system performance remains poorly understood. This gap is critical, as architectural choices alone can induce order-of-magnitude differences in latency and throughput, as well as substantial variation in accuracy and scalability.
%
Addressing this challenge requires (i) jointly evaluating multiple capabilities, such as orchestration overhead, memory behavior, planning, specialization, and coordination, and (ii) conducting these evaluations under controlled, framework-level conditions to isolate architectural effects. Existing benchmarks focus on individual capabilities and lack standardized framework-level evaluation.
%
We address these limitations by (i) introducing an architectural taxonomy for systematically comparing multi-agent LLM frameworks along fundamental dimensions, and (ii) developing MAFBench, a unified evaluation suite that integrates existing benchmarks under a standardized execution pipeline. Using MAFBench, we conduct a controlled empirical study across several widely used frameworks. Our results show that \textbf{framework-level design choices alone can increase latency by over $100\times$, reduce planning accuracy by up to $30\%$, and lower coordination success from above $90\%$ to below $30\%$.} Finally, we translate our findings into concrete architectural design principles and framework-selection guidance, and outline promising future research directions.

\end{abstract}

% Despite rapid adoption of large language model (LLM)–based multi-agent frameworks, there is no principled way to reason about their architectural trade-offs or to compare frameworks at the system level. This lack of structure obscures fundamental constraints and leads to ad hoc design choices. We identify three dominant multi-agent framework paradigms, graph-based orchestration, role-based agent pipelines, and generative agent-based modeling (GABM), and introduce a unifying, framework-agnostic taxonomy that provides a common vocabulary for reasoning about agent structure, memory, planning, communication, and collaboration. Using this taxonomy as a foundation, we develop MAFBench, a standardized evaluation suite that enables controlled, framework-level analysis across aligned execution and measurement protocols. Our study reveals a set of irreducible design laws rather than isolated performance effects. Lightweight graph-based and role-based frameworks scale predictably with modest overhead, whereas feature-rich role-based designs incur substantial latency, and simulation-centric generative agent architectures operate in a fundamentally slower regime. We show that specialization is driven by procedural guidance rather than role identity, that free-form planning preserves robustness while schema-constrained planning degrades reliability, and that coordination outcomes are governed primarily by communication topology, with no universally optimal structure. Finally, we demonstrate that current agent memory designs, based on retrieval or short-term context accumulation, fail to support dependency-aware updates and selective forgetting, establishing fundamental limitations that cannot be overcome by prompt engineering or scale alone.
