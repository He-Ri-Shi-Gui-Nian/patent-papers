% New Abstract
%As large language models (LLMs) advance, their potential to serve multicultural communities underscores the critical need for equitable cultural alignment. 
Large Language Models (LLMs) need to adapt their predictions to diverse cultural contexts to benefit diverse communities across the world. While previous efforts have focused on single-LLM, single-turn approaches, we propose to exploit the complementary strengths of multiple LLMs to promote cultural adaptability. We introduce a Multi-Agent Debate framework, where two LLM-based agents debate over a cultural scenario and collaboratively reach a final decision. We propose two variants: one where either LLM agents exclusively debate and another where they dynamically choose between self-reflection and debate during their turns. We evaluate these approaches on 7 open-weight LLMs (and 21 LLM combinations)  using the \textsc{NormAd-eti} benchmark for social etiquette norms in 75 countries. Experiments show that debate improves both overall accuracy and cultural group parity over single-LLM baselines. Notably, multi-agent debate enables relatively small LLMs (7-9\textsc{B}) to achieve accuracies comparable to that of a much larger model (27\textsc{B} parameters).\footnote{We release our code and dataset at \url{https://github.com/dayeonki/cultural_debate}.}

Our findings highlight the potential of multi-LLM collaboration to enhance equitable cultural alignment\footnote{All the code and data will be made publicly available upon publication.}.

\section{Choice of Judge LLM}
\label{appendix:judge_llm}
We detail the selection process for our judge LLM in the multi-LLM setup in Table \ref{tab:judge_llm}. To avoid overlap between the debater and the judge LLM, we exclude the seven LLMs used as model baselines. We also exclude \textsc{GPT-4} \cite{gpt4}, which was used in constructing the \textsc{NormAd-eti} benchmark. Our judge LLM candidates include three open-weight LLMs (\textsc{Gemma-2-27b}, \textsc{LLaMA-3-70b}, and \textsc{Yi-1.5-34b}) and one proprietary LLM (\textsc{ChatGPT}\footnote{\url{https://openai.com/index/chatgpt/}}). We selected the open-weight LLMs from model families where their smaller 7-9B variants achieved the highest single-LLM baseline scores (Table \ref{tab:single_llm_new}). From these candidates, we evaluate their single-LLM baseline accuracies and choose \textsc{Gemma-2-27b} as the judge LLM for its high mean accuracy and efficient inference time.

\subsection{Pre- vs. Post-Alignment Models}
\label{appendix:pre_post_alignment}
Our initial baselines focus on instruction fine-tuned models, reflecting the findings from \textsc{NormAd-eti} \citep{normad} of the impact of different optimization methods on the cultural adaptability of LLMs, including PPO \citep{schulman2017proximalpolicyoptimizationalgorithms} and DPO \citep{rafailov2024directpreferenceoptimizationlanguage} on SFT \textsc{LLaMA-1} models \citep{touvron2023llamaopenefficientfoundation}. Their findings showed that PPO and DPO achieved higher accuracy compared to SFT for the 7\textsc{B} variant.

We extend our analysis to include an additional model baseline and its counterpart: \textsc{OLMo-Instruct 7B} and \textsc{OLMo-SFT 7B} \citep{groeneveld-etal-2024-olmo}. As shown in Table \ref{tab:training_methods}, post-alignment models consistently outperform SFT in terms of average accuracy for the single LLM baseline, both with and without the rule-of-thumb (RoT) information, confirming the choice of baselines in our experiments.

\subsection{Does Increasing Rounds of Debate Help?}
\label{appendix:increasing_rounds}
Our default multi-agent debate framework involves a single exchange of feedback between two LLM agents, which we define as one \textit{round} of debate. To explore whether increasing the number of debate rounds can enhance mean accuracy, we test one of the best-performing LLM combination in Debate-Only setup (\textsc{LLaMA-3}+\textsc{Gemma-2}) across 1 to 5 rounds.\footnote{We chose this LLM combination since it shows the fastest inference speed.} Results for individual and final debate accuracies are present in Table \ref{tab:debate_rounds}. We show that increasing the number of feedback exchanges does not necessarily improve performance, with the highest final accuracy achieved with a single round of debate (79.7\%). Additionally, we observe that the decreased accuracy of \textsc{LLaMA-3} in larger number of rounds is primarily due to cases where the LLM falls into dead loops (\textit{e.g.,} failing to progress the discussion after several turns). In sum, we opt for a single round of debate in our multi-agent debate setup, prioritizing efficient inference over the marginal accuracy gains for \textsc{Gemma-2}.

\subsection{Does Increasing Iterations of Self-Reflection Help?}
\label{appendix:increasing_sr}
We extend the Self-Reflection approach by increasing the number of iterations ($N$) to 2 and 3, using the best-performing Debate-Only baseline models, \textsc{LLaMA-3} and \textsc{Gemma-2}. For $N$=2 and 3, we append the history of all prior self-reflections in the prompts. As shown in Table \ref{tab:increasing_sr}, the accuracy Self-Reflection increases as $N$ increases. However, for all iterations ($N$=1,2,3), the accuracies remain lower than that of the multi-agent debate between \textsc{LLaMA-3} and \textsc{Gemma-2} (79.7\%). Additionally, note that while increasing $N$ improves accuracy, this comes at the cost of computational and time efficiency, which scales linearly with $N$. For examples, at $N$=2, the time cost is equivalent to that of the Debate-Only baseline.

\section{Role of Judge LLM}
\label{appendix:role_judge_llm}
To understand the role of the judge LLM in multi-agent debate, we test two variants: \textbf{1) Oracle:} when the two agents disagree, we use the ground truth label to select the correct decision from the two. \textbf{2) Random:} when the two debater agents $\mathcal{M}_1$ and $\mathcal{M}_2$ disagree, we randomly select from the two agents' final decisions. Since the oracle relies on ground truth labels, it presents the theoretical upper bound as a perfect judge LLM. We show results for Debate-Only and Self-Reflect+Debate in Tables \ref{tab:role_d} and \ref{tab:role_sd}, respectively. 

For Debate-Only, we observe that the mean accuracy difference between the oracle (\textbf{Oracle}) and the final debate accuracies (\textbf{D}) is 3.6\%, compared to an 8.0\% difference between the oracle and the random baseline (\textbf{Random}). This demonstrates that using \textsc{Gemma-2-7b} as the judge LLM effectively narrows the gap with the oracle, highlighting the unique advantages of multi-agent debate over the random baseline. Similar trends are observed in the Self-Reflect+Debate setup, where the accuracy difference between the oracle and final debate accuracies (\textbf{S+D}) is 3.7\%, while the difference with the random baseline is 10.8\%. 

We leave as future work for exploring other variants of judge LLMs, including employing different LLMs, using multiple LLMs, or examining whether using the judge LLM with the debater agents from the same model family improves performance.
