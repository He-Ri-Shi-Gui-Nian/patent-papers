%As large language models (LLMs) continue to advance, their potential to serve multicultural and global communities becomes increasingly apparent. This highlights the growing importance of ensuring that LLMs are accessible and effective across diverse cultural contexts to support equitable deployment. 

Large Language Models (LLMs) should be able to adapt their predictions to distinct cultural contexts to benefit diverse communities across the world. Recent efforts in this direction have focused on aligning LLMs to reflect diverse human values and norms across cultures \cite{modular, choenni-etal-2024-echoes, culturellm, selfalignment}. However, these efforts have focused on single-LLM, single-turn generation approaches \cite{cao-etal-2023-assessing, investigating, xu2024selfpluralisingculturealignmentlarge}. Training specialized LLMs \cite{culturellm} and prompt design \cite{shen-etal-2024-understanding} have been shown to improve overall cultural alignment. Yet it can be expected that data selection and model design decisions make it challenging for any single model to support all cultures equally well. 

Meanwhile, a wealth of open-weight LLMs have been released, with varying training data distributions, alignment processes, and language coverage \cite{yu2023kola, du2023improving, bansal2024llm}, thus offering the potential for complementary  perspectives and reasoning paths~\cite{farperspective, liang-etal-2024-encouraging}. Interactions between such models may lead to more accurate understanding and evaluation of culturally sensitive scenarios, which motivates us to explore multi-LLM collaboration-based approaches. 

How can multiple LLMs collaborate toward equitable alignment across cultures? We investigate a common form of multi-LLM collaboration: debate \cite{irving2018ai, khan2024debatingpersuasivellmsleads, kenton2024scalableoversightweakllms}. We propose a \textbf{Multi-Agent Debate} framework, where two LLM agents debate over the given scenario and collaboratively arrive at a final decision with a judge LLM. We introduce two key variants as illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:main_figure}: \textbf{1) Debate-Only}, where multiple LLM agents exclusively engage in debate with a discussant, and \textbf{2) Self-Reflect+Debate}, where each LLM agent dynamically choose between self-reflection and debating during its turn. To conduct a comprehensive comparison study, we investigate two additional strategies based on single-LLM in Figure \ref{fig:main_figure}: 3) Single Model, where a single LLM generates outputs, and 4) Self-Reflection, where an LLM generates verbal self-reflections on its own outputs and incorporate them in subsequent iterations.

Using the \textsc{NormAd-eti} benchmark \cite{normad}, which probes social-etiquette related social norms across 75 countries, we evaluate the four above strategies across 7 LLMs (and 21 LLM combinations). We begin by improving single-LLM performance through cultural contextualization and self-reflection (\S \ref{sec:single_model}), and show that the best-performing LLM often varies across cultures (\S \ref{sec:oracle}), which motivates the multi-agent debate setup (\S \ref{sec:debate_only}, \S \ref{sec:selfreflect_debate}). As we will see, using multi-turn multi-agent approaches improve over single-turn single-LLM accuracies.  We also analyze how predictions evolve throughout the debate stages: while the  LLM agents often disagree initially (\S \ref{sec:decision_dynamics}), debate effectively guides the agents to revise these decisions, with most revisions resulting in correct final outcomes. Moreover, we demonstrate that improvements are consistent across cultural groups, with the multi-agent debate setup achieving the highest parity in performance, particularly benefiting underrepresented cultural groups (\S \ref{sec:cultural_group_parity}).

% Language inherently reflects the cultural values of the people who speak it \cite{kendall2007sociology, hovy2021importance}. Consequently, large language models (LLMs), pre-trained on vast datasets in diverse languages, must be aligned to serve pluralistic human values across cultures \cite{modular, xu2024selfpluralisingculturealignmentlarge}. In this context, we define \textbf{cultural alignment} as the ability to generate responses that reflect and respect the cultural norms and values of its target audience, leading to more inclusive and effective representation of the evolving culture \cite{hershcovich-etal-2022-challenges}. For instance, it is essential to understand that ``\textit{waiting for the eldest person to begin eating before starting meal}'' is socially acceptable in South Korea, while ``\textit{directly handing a gift to the host during a visit}'' is socially inappropriate in Afghanistan.

% How can we effectively steer LLMs toward equitable cultural alignment across different countries and languages? Recent efforts have sought to align LLM behavior with human preferences, steering models to generate responses that better reflect human values \cite{cao-etal-2023-assessing, choenni-etal-2024-echoes, culturellm, investigating, selfalignment}. While training an LLM or delicate designing the prompts may strengthen the alignment of a model to certain cultures, a single model is usually challenging to cover all the cultures equally. For example, it is still an open question whether these approaches bring equitable gains to highly developed and marginalized countries. In contrast, different LLMs can provide diverse perspectives and alternate reasoning paths aligning with separate cultures~\cite{du2023improving, farperspective, liang-etal-2024-encouraging}, and their interactions may lead to more accurate understanding and evaluation of culture-sensitive scenarios.

To provide a comprehensive understanding of strategies for culturally aligning LLMs, we define five methods ranging from single to multi-LLM setups, as illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:main_figure}: \textbf{(1) Single Model}, where a single LLM generates outputs, \textbf{(2) Self-Reflection}, where LLMs generate verbal self-reflections on their outputs and incorporate them in subsequent iterations. \textbf{(3) Selective Model}, where predictions for each culture are routed to the model with higher mean accuracy (we use this as a theoretical upper bound for using multiple LLMs). \textbf{(4) Multi-Agent Debate}, where two LLM-based agents debate over the given scenario and collaboratively arrive at a final decision with a judge LLM.

A primary observation is that the multi-agent debate outperforms single LLM baselines in 19 out of 21 model combinations, achieving a significant improvement of 7.05\% on average in terms of mean accuracy. Further analysis reveals that self-reflection and multi-agent debate are particularly effective in enhancing model predictions for cultural adaptability in scenarios that neither strictly adhere to nor violate the rule of relevance (\S \ref{sec:decision_dynamics}), addressing inherent agreement biases in LLMs \cite{perez2022discovering, huang2024trustllm}. Moreover, these strategies help improve cultural group parity, especially for marginalized cultural groups (\S \ref{sec:cultural_group_parity}).
