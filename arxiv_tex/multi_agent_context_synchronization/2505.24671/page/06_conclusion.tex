\section{Conclusion}


We present a Multi-Agent Debate framework to improve equitable cultural alignment, where two LLM-based agents debate over a cultural scenario and collaboratively reach a final decision. We explore two variants: \textbf{1)} Debate-Only, where models exclusively engage in debate with the discussant, and \textbf{2)} Self-Reflect-Debate, where models dynamically choose between self-reflection and debate.

Evaluating 7 LLMs on the \textsc{NormAd-eti} benchmark, we first maximize the single-LLM performance through cultural contextualization and self-reflection (\S \ref{sec:single_model}), and show the potential of combining LLMs in an oracle setting (\S \ref{sec:oracle}). %the best-performing LLM often varies across cultures and different LLMs have the potential to complement each other, which
This motivates our multi-turn, multi-LLM approach. Both variants of our multi-agent debate framework show improvements in individual debate accuracies for 20 and 14 out of 21 settings respectively (\S \ref{sec:debate_only}, \S \ref{sec:selfreflect_debate}), with debate decision dynamics  (\S \ref{sec:decision_dynamics}) leading to more equitable benefits across cultural groups (\S \ref{sec:cultural_group_parity}). %This also effectively resolves disagreed initial decisions between two agents, mostly leading to both correct outcomes (\S \ref{sec:decision_dynamics}). Beyond average accuracy improvements, we show that debate consistently benefits across cultural groups, showing higher parity compared to single-LLM and other multi-LLM methods (\S \ref{sec:cultural_group_parity}). 
Notably, multi-agent debate enables 7-9B LLMs to achieve performance comparable to a much larger LLM \textsc{Gemma-2-27B} (79.2\%).

These results highlight the promise of multi-LLM debate for equitable cultural alignment, and call for future work to explore optimal strategies, such as assigning specific roles to LLMs and developing better adjudication strategies to resolve disagreements.
%Taken together, our work highlights promising directions for future research on multi-LLM collaboration for equitable cultural alignment. Our Multi-Agent Debate framework represents an initial step in this direction, with future efforts needed to explore optimal strategies, such as assigning specific roles to LLMs, developing methods to aggregate decisions when debaters disagree, or introducing multiple judge LLMs.
