\section{Dataset Construction}

\label{sec:dataset}

Below we provide the details of generating three datasets for experiments. Traditional question-answering benchmarks such as Natural Questions (NQ)\mcite{kwiatkowski2019natural}, HotpotQA\mcite{yang2018hotpotqa}, and MS-MARCO\mcite{nguyen2016ms} are not suitable for \rag experiment because of their discrete knowledge fragments. Hence, there is a need for datasets that incorporate graph structures to fully exercise \rag's capabilities. Besides, sensitive datasets are preferred to highlight the potential risks of \attack.




{\bf Geographic Dataset.} Geographic knowledge is suitable for \rag as there are a great number of subordinate relations within it. For example, a country has multiple states and a state has multiple cities. Such commonsense knowledge is widely used for building question-answering systems\mcite{soto-jimenezRAGBasedQuestionAnsweringSystems2024, liHarnessingLargeLanguage2024} and knowledge graph research\mcite{ilievskiCSKGCommonSenseKnowledge2021,wangWikiGraphsWikipediaText2021}.

Therefore, we gathered information from Wikipedia at different geographic levels, including \emph{continents, countries, states, cities, and landmarks}, to create the Geographic Dataset. We manually selected entries with hierarchical relationships to ensure the dataset accurately reflects geographic dependencies that can be captured by \rag. Next, we employed automated scripts to extract textual information from Wikipedia pages.




{\bf Medical Dataset.} Hetionet\mcite{HetionetGithub} is an existing medical knowledge graph that can assist clinical decision-making\mcite{santosKnowledgeGraphInterpret2022} and has been widely adopted in knowledge graph research\mcite{hildebrandtReasoningKnowledgeGraphs2020,xi2023security}. We collect the information of \emph{disease, symptom, anatomical location, medicine, side effect of medicine} to form our dataset. Specifically, to obtain a subgraph, we randomly selected several diseases and retrieved their connected entities, including \emph{symptoms, anatomical locations, medicines, and side effects of medicines}. We generated textual data based on these entities and their relations using predefined templates as follows:

{\bf Query Generation.} We utilize LLMs to generate multi-hop queries and their corresponding answers by randomly sampling entities and relations from \rag's knowledge graph. Here's our detailed approach:

First, we use the original dataset as a knowledge base and have \rag perform indexing to construct a knowledge graph. We then randomly \yh{select one entity} from this knowledge graph and traverse through relations to find connected entities. Specifically, we follow one relation to reach a connected entity, then follow another relation from that entity to reach the next one.

This process creates a path containing multiple entities and relations. We then task the LLM to formulate questions based on this path. The questions are constructed with several constraints: the initial entity can appear in the question, while the final entity in the path serves as the answer. Importantly, intermediate entities must not be mentioned in the question. All questions must follow natural and logical reasoning patterns, avoiding any queries that might seem unusual or counterintuitive.





Since the experiment primarily serves as a prototype demonstration, we used a subset of data from three sources to construct the dataset following normal deep learning settings \mcite{lu2025journey,wangtase2025,lu2025predicting,zheng2025machine,wang2025selfdestructivelanguagemodel,niu2025decoding}.
